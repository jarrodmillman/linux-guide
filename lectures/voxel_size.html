What is the voxel size of my image?Â¶

A voxel is an element in the 3D image array.

We can think of the voxel as representing a volume that is a 3D rectangle (rectangular box).

The lengths of the sides of the box in millimeters are the voxel sizes. The voxel sizes will be a length 3 vector.

This page describes how to work out the voxel sizes from the image affine.

To understand this page, you should first read coordinate systems and affines.

The voxel size of an array axis \(d\) is the distance in millimeters moved when moving from the center of one voxel to the next voxel on axis \(d\).

For example, I could start at the voxel of coordinate (array index) \((0, 0, 0)\). The voxel size for the first axis (\(v_1\)) is the distance in millimeters moved when moving from \((0, 0, 0)\) to \((1, 0, 0)\).

Remember that the affine gives the millimeter coordinate corresponding to any given voxel (array) coordinate.

The image affine \(A\) can be written as:

\[\begin{split}A = \begin{bmatrix} m_{11} & m_{12} & m_{13} & a \\ m_{21} & m_{22} & m_{23} & b \\ m_{31} & m_{32} & m_{33} & c \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

If I apply the image affine to the voxel coordinate \((0, 0, 0)\) I get:

\[\begin{split}\begin{bmatrix} x\\ y\\ z\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} m_{11} & m_{12} & m_{13} & a \\ m_{21} & m_{22} & m_{23} & b \\ m_{31} & m_{32} & m_{33} & c \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ 0\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Thus:

\[\begin{split}x = a \\ y = b \\ z = c\end{split}\]

Applying the image affine to voxel coordinate \((1, 0, 0)\) I get:

\[\begin{split}\begin{bmatrix} x'\\ y'\\ z'\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} m_{11} & m_{12} & m_{13} & a \\ m_{21} & m_{22} & m_{23} & b \\ m_{31} & m_{32} & m_{33} & c \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 0\\ 0\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

So:

\[\begin{split}x' = m_{11} + a \\ y' = m_{21} + b \\ z' = m_{31} + c\end{split}\]

The difference vector moving from \((0, 0, 0)\) to \((1, 0, 0)\) is therefore:

\[\begin{split}\begin{bmatrix} d_x\\ d_y\\ d_z\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x'\\ y'\\ z'\\ \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} x\\ y\\ z\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} m_{11}\\ m_{21}\\ m_{31} \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

The Euclidean length of this difference vector is the voxel size for the first image axis:

\[v_1 = \sqrt{m_{11}^2 + m_{21}^2 + m_{31}^2}\]

By following the same logic, moving from \((0, 0, 0)\) to \((0, 1, 0)\) or \((0, 0, 1)\), you can show that the voxel sizes for the second and third axes are the Euclidean lengths of the second and third columns of the image affine:

\[\begin{split}v_2 = \sqrt{m_{12}^2 + m_{22}^2 + m_{32}^2} \\ v_3 = \sqrt{m_{13}^2 + m_{23}^2 + m_{33}^2}\end{split}\]
